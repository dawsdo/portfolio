I built this for my dad. He had a seizure that left him with aphasia and partial paralysis on one side. The walking stick, the slow speech, the way half his face stays still when he tries to smile — none of it announced itself in advance. I started the Seizure Diary because the people I love deserve better tools for understanding the things that happen to them.

## The problem with existing health logs

Most seizure tracking happens in two forms today: paper notebooks that get lost, and consumer wellness apps that treat seizures like a wellness metric next to steps and sleep score. Neither is right. Paper has no structure for later analysis. Consumer apps have structure but no respect — they don't ask the questions a neurologist would, and they pump the data through advertising and insurance pipelines that patients shouldn't have to consent to in order to track their own body.

A purpose-built platform sits between those two extremes. Structured enough that patterns can emerge over time. Private enough that the data belongs to the patient, not a vendor. That's what I wanted to build.

## What I built (v1)

The current platform is a Next.js application with PostgreSQL persistence via Prisma ORM and OAuth 2.0 authentication. Patients can sign in, log seizure events with structured fields (timestamp, duration, symptoms, location), and add free-text notes per event. Each user owns their own data — there's no shared visibility yet, no caregiver or provider roles. That's intentional for v1: get the foundation right before introducing the complexity of multi-role access.

## The deep dive: schema design for health data

The most interesting engineering decision was the data model. Health data has properties that ordinary CRUD apps don't share. Events are time-series but also have nested optional fields. Some events are tightly structured (duration in minutes, symptoms from a controlled vocabulary), others are unstructured (a free-text note describing what the patient felt that the form didn't capture). The schema has to accept both shapes cleanly and let analytics layer on top without contortion.

I designed the Prisma schema around a `User` and an `Event` table, where `Event` has required structured fields and optional unstructured ones. Symptoms are stored as a typed enum rather than a free string, which costs me one extra step at form-input time but pays back when I later want to aggregate or correlate events programmatically. Free-text notes live in a separate field with no parser constraints — patients can write whatever they need, and the structured fields stay clean.

Prisma's type generation turned out to be the right call for this domain. Health data should not be silently coerced. A `null` symptom field and an empty string are different — one means "not recorded," the other means "actively recorded as nothing." Prisma's generated types enforce that distinction at compile time, which catches bugs I wouldn't catch reviewing my own code.

## What's next

The closest-to-shippable next feature is environmental trigger correlation. Once a patient has logged enough events, the platform should be able to surface correlations — "in the past 90 days, events tend to cluster on days when you reported less than 6 hours of sleep the night before." That's a real analytical capability, not a visualization, and it's the feature that turns the diary from a *record* into a *tool*. The schema is built to support this; the analytics layer isn't written yet.

Past that, the roadmap includes patient-caregiver shared visibility (real RBAC, not the per-user isolation I have now), trends visualization, and clinician-friendly export. I'd rather ship correlations well than ship all four features poorly.

## What I'd do differently

Two things. First, I'd treat the schema design as a separate phase from the application build, not interleaved with it. I made a few schema decisions early that I'd undo now — small things, like representing duration as an integer of minutes instead of a more flexible interval type. Schema migrations are doable but they're work, and a clean upfront design pass would have saved me from the cleanup.

Second, I'd write more tests against the auth boundary specifically. The hardest bugs to catch in a system like this are the ones where a user can accidentally see data that isn't theirs. I tested manually and it works, but a dedicated test suite around per-user data isolation would let me sleep better at night.
